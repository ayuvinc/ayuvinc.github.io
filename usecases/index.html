Out-patient clinic · a routine script
The problem

A handwritten script.
No checks. Two people exposed.

The patient is allergic to penicillin and on warfarin — but the doctor can't see it, and the pharmacist can't read the scrawl. An illegible, unchecked prescription puts both of them on the hook.

The lesson: bad handwriting isn't just unsafe — it's a DPDP accuracy liability. An inaccurate record acted on downstream is a legal failure, and it lands on the doctor and the pharmacist.

Health AI · trained on what, exactly?
The problem

Your record trained a model.
You never said yes.

Most health AI is built on records used for care and then quietly reused for training — with no separate opt-in. When enforcement lands, that corpus becomes a liability, not an asset.

The lesson: DPDP treats AI-training as its own purpose. The real moat isn't how much data you have — it's whether the patient consented to that exact use.
